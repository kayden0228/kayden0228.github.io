Published · January 2024

Common Features of Environmental Mycobacterium chelonae from Colorado Using Partial and Whole Genomic Sequence Analyses

Current Microbiology, 2024 Jan 18; 81(2):69  ·  DOI: 10.1007/s00284-023-03589-2

Kayden G. Glauser, Reagan E. Kelley, William J. Leonard, Jo Hendrix, Suzanne Petri, Eric I. Tong, Yvonne L. Chan, Ettie M. Lipner, Stephanie N. Dawrs, Jennifer R. Honda

Research Overview

Nontuberculous mycobacteria (NTM) are naturally occurring bacteria found in environmental sources such as soil, water, and biofilms. Mycobacterium chelonae is a rapidly growing NTM species increasingly associated with skin, soft tissue, and pulmonary infections in humans — yet much remains unknown about its environmental reservoirs and genomic diversity.

This study analyzed environmental isolates of M. chelonae collected from Colorado, employing both partial gene sequencing and whole genomic sequencing (WGS) to identify common genomic features across strains. By characterizing the genetic structure of these environmental isolates, the research contributes to a broader understanding of NTM epidemiology, regional distribution, and the relationship between environmental and clinical strains — with important implications for public health surveillance and infection prevention.
